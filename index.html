Math riddle: two bacteria in a bucket

Start with one A and one B bacterium. Each food particle is eaten by a random bacterium in proportion to its share; the eater reproduces. After N drops, what is the distribution of A?

Show solution

This is a classic Pólya urn process. Starting with 1 A and 1 B, after N drops the number of A is uniformly distributed over \(1,2,\dots,N+1\). Equivalently, the fraction of A converges in distribution to \(\mathrm{Beta}(1,1)\), i.e., uniform on \([0,1]\).

Math conjecture: High‑water marks

For a sequence with running maxima, the high‑water marks are the values where the running maximum increases. Over all permutations of \(n\) elements, the distribution of the number of high‑water marks equals the distribution of the number of cycles in a permutation, given by the unsigned Stirling numbers of the first kind \(c(n,k)\).

The probability mass function is \(\Pr(K=k) = c(n,k)/n!\), where \(K\) is the number of high‑water marks.

(Sketch: use the bijection between permutations and their cycle structure; it preserves counts with respect to record values.)
